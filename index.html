The United States did not avoid the tragedies of thalidomide

The United States is colloquially known as the grand exception to the 1960s thalidomide scandal, but about 100 self-reported survivors are still living in the states.

Medicine companies marketed thalidomide as a catch-all: sleep-aid, nausea relief, morning sickness relief, headaches, and more. Expecting mothers thought it was safe – they had no reason to think otherwise. But when taken during pregnancy, thalidomide gravely harms the fetus. An unknown number of babies were stillborn, and estimates for worldwide victims are up to 150,000 people, accounting for unreported miscarriages.

 

 

Thalidomide is a drug originally created by the German company Grüenthal after the second World War. It was created as a weight-loss drug, but when tested on mice it appeared as a depressant drug that could be used for sleep. The company switched gears, and human testing officially began soon after. The drug was only tested on men before it was sent out to the market.

 

 

Thalidomide made it to the United States for human testing a year later in 1956. The next month, a baby with no ears was born to a Grüenthal employee back in Germany.

 

 

Scientists now know that thalidomide causes fatal or life-altering birth defects when taken during pregnancy, but it would take five years before the drug was pulled from shelves in Germany – and another year before the United States would take action in 1962.

 

 

No one person will ever have a surefire number of American children harmed or killed by thalidomide. Those with the best understanding are dying out with time, leaving the grown children of thalidomide to fight for themselves.
